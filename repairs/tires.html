🏎️ Tire Replacements

17 years. 250,000+ miles. Six sets of tires. What actually lasted — and what it cost.

🟢 Easy (no DIY needed) 📐 Size: P185/65R-15 📍 South Florida 💰 $340–$632 installed
Home › Repairs › Tires
Prius tire size: P185/65R-15. All Gen 2 Prius (2004–2009) use this size. Low rolling resistance matters on a Prius — the right tire improves fuel economy; the wrong one hurts it noticeably.

What the Data Shows

📊

Average Lifespan: ~42,000 miles

Across five completed sets, tires lasted between 29,000 and 56,000 miles. Florida heat, highway driving, and Prius regen braking all affect wear.

🌧️

Wet Weather Is the Real Test

Every set started slipping in rain before the tread technically wore out. In Florida, wet traction degrades well before you reach the wear bars. Replace when wet grip drops — don't wait for the tread indicator.

🏪

Costco Was Usually the Best Deal

Costco pricing (including installation) beat most competitors after rebates. The membership pays for itself on one tire purchase if you weren't already a member.

⚠️

Warranty Miles Are Optimistic

Every set was warranted for 70k–80k miles. None lasted that long in real-world South Florida use. Don't buy tires based on warranty mileage alone.

Rotation & Maintenance

Rotate every 5,000–7,500 miles on a Prius. Regenerative braking puts more wear on the front tires than a conventional car. Skipping rotations will cut tire life significantly — the front tires will wear out while the rears still have tread.
  • Prius uses a front-heavy regen braking bias — front tires wear faster than rear
  • Tire pressure: 35 PSI front and rear (check cold). The Prius TPMS will alert at ~28 PSI but don't wait for that
  • The Gen 2 TPMS monitors pressure only — it does not tell you which tire is low until it's significantly underinflated
  • Check pressure monthly in Florida — heat causes pressure to fluctuate more than in cooler climates

Notes from the Field

Over 17 years, tires were the most frequent recurring expense on this car — more than any single repair. The pattern was consistent: good grip for the first 30,000 miles, acceptable grip to about 40,000, then a noticeable drop in wet-weather confidence in Florida rain.

The Bridgestone Ecopia EP422+ was the best for fuel economy — it's a true low-rolling-resistance tire and you can feel the difference in highway MPG. The Michelins lasted longer and had better wet grip. The Goodyears were fine but nothing special.

For anyone still driving a high-mileage Gen 2 in Florida: check your tires in the wet season. A 45,000-mile tire in South Florida heat and rain is not the same as one in a drier climate.
